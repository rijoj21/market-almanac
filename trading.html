Plate VI · The fast lane of the markets

Trading — reading
the market's pulse.

Investing is the slow lane; trading is the fast one. Where an investor plants money and waits years, a trader buys and sells over months, days, or even seconds, trying to profit from price moves. It has its own charts, its own vocabulary, and its own very real dangers.

⚠️

Read this first: trading is genuinely hard and high-risk. Study after study finds that the majority of active retail traders lose money over time, and brokers in many countries are legally required to warn that most accounts lose on leveraged products. This page explains how trading works so you understand it — it is not a suggestion to do it, and nothing here is advice or a strategy to follow.

The idea

What is trading?

Trading is buying and selling assets to profit from short-term price changes. A trader doesn't necessarily care whether a company is great for the next decade — they care whether its price will move in the next hour, day or week. The same shares, currencies, commodities or coins can be both invested in and traded; the difference is the clock and the intent.

Investors ask, "Is this a good business to own for years?" Traders ask, "Which way will the price move next, and how do I manage the risk if I'm wrong?" Most traders lean on charts — the visual history of a price — to spot patterns, rather than studying company fundamentals. That whole approach is called technical analysis.

Reading a chart

The candlestick, decoded

Most price charts are built from candlesticks. Each candle sums up one slice of time (a minute, an hour, a day) into four numbers — the open, high, low and close — and its colour tells you, at a glance, whether the price rose or fell.

One candle = one period. The thick "body" spans open-to-close; the thin "wicks" mark the highest and lowest prices touched.
The vocabulary, drawn

Patterns traders watch for

A handful of ideas come up again and again. None of them predict the future — they're just a shared language for describing what a price is doing.

Trend — which way is it leaning?

Support & resistance — the floor and ceiling

Prices often stall and bounce at the same levels. Break cleanly through one and traders watch for the move to continue.

Bid / Ask

The bid is the most a buyer will pay; the ask is the least a seller will take. The gap is the spread — a hidden cost on every trade.

Volume

How many units changed hands. A big move on high volume is taken more seriously than one on thin volume.

Liquidity

How easily you can buy or sell without moving the price. Thin, illiquid markets are dangerous to trade.

Moving Average

The average price over the last N periods, drawn as a smooth line to reveal the underlying direction through the noise.

Breakout

When price pushes decisively past a support or resistance level — often where traders expect a bigger move.

Leverage

Borrowing to trade a bigger position than your cash allows. It magnifies gains and losses — the fastest way to blow up an account.

Managing the downside

Risk tools: stop-loss & take-profit

Serious traders obsess over risk management, not profits. Before entering, they decide where they'll get out if wrong (a stop-loss) and where they'll take gains (a take-profit). Comparing the two gives a risk-to-reward ratio — a way to size whether a trade is even worth taking.

A stop-loss caps how much you can lose; position sizing keeps any single trade from sinking the account.
Approaches

Common trading styles

Traders are mostly sorted by their time horizon — how long they hold a position. Faster styles mean more trades, more costs, more stress, and more chances to be wrong. These are descriptions, not recommendations.

Seconds–minutes

Scalping

Dozens or hundreds of tiny trades a day, each chasing a sliver of profit. Demands intense focus, speed and very low fees.

Risk: costs and small mistakes pile up fast; brutal mentally.
Within one day

Day Trading

Opening and closing all positions the same day, never holding overnight. A full-time job that lives on charts and news.

Risk: the style where the most beginners lose money.
Days–weeks

Swing Trading

Holding for a few days to weeks to catch a single "swing" in price. Slower-paced, so it suits people with day jobs.

Risk: overnight news can gap the price against you.
Weeks–months

Trend Following

Spotting a clear up- or down-trend and riding it until it bends, rather than predicting tops and bottoms.

Risk: whipsaws in sideways markets cause repeated small losses.
Months–years

Position Trading

The slowest trading style — holding for months on a big-picture view. It starts to blur into long-term investing.

Risk: a long wrong call ties up money and conviction.
Years+

…vs. Investing

For contrast: buy good assets and hold for years, ignoring the noise. Lower stress, lower costs, and historically kinder to most people.

The Almanac's whole spirit — patience over prediction.
The part nobody likes

The risks — honestly

The hard truth

Most people who actively trade lose money. It is a competitive arena full of professionals, algorithms and institutions with better data, lower costs and no emotions. Treating it as a fast route to wealth is how savings disappear.

  • 01Leverage amplifies losses. Borrowed money can wipe out your stake — and sometimes more than you put in — in a single move.
  • 02Emotions sabotage you. Fear, greed, FOMO and "revenge trading" after a loss push people into their worst decisions.
  • 03Costs quietly bleed you. Spreads, commissions, overnight fees and slippage take a cut of every trade, win or lose.
  • 04The market can stay irrational. Being "right" eventually is no comfort if a move stops you out first.
  • 05It demands time and nerves. Active trading is stressful, time-hungry work — not passive income.
  • 06Beware the hype. "Signals," courses and gurus promising easy profits are usually selling you something, not edge.
